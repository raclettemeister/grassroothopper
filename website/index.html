01

What is grassroot hopping?

All over the world, people are building open-source alternatives to the platforms that run our lives. Cooperative delivery apps. Community-owned music streaming. Federated social networks. Open-source event tools.

Grassroot hopping is the act of taking the best of what already exists and assembling it into something communities can own. Not building everything from scratch — catching everything that's already being built.

02

The first product: a local social network owned by its members.

Imagine a social network for your city — but without the corporation behind it. No ads, no algorithm, no data harvesting. Just the creative people in your neighborhood and the events happening around you.

You join through a community you already belong to — your dance scene, your art class, your coworking space, your running club. Through that community, you discover the hidden creative lives of the people you already know.

The Brussels network is the first. The code is open source. Any city can fork it and start their own. The name gets decided by the cooperative members — because they own it.

03

Here's what it actually looks like.

You go to a Lindy Hop social on a Thursday night. Someone holds up a QR code and says the password out loud. You scan it on your phone. Pick a username. You're in.

You scroll through the community and see only familiar faces. The woman you dance with every week? She's a painter. The guy who runs the Sunday social? He writes poetry. The couple who teach beginner workshops? They organize indie film screenings.

There's no comment button. No way to message them through the app. It's purposefully slow — near-analog. You see their art, and then you wait until the next social to walk up and say: “I love your last pictures.” Or you send them a postcard. The connection happens in real life, at human speed.

04

Who is this great for?

Readers
You discover the creative lives of people you already know. Every event in one place. No ads, no noise. Just your community.
Creators
You're a painter who also runs with a club, takes dance classes, and goes to poetry nights. You're visible in all three communities. People who already know your face see your art — and become real fans. They buy you a coffee. They buy a print. No algorithm needed. You thrive by simply being connected.
Event planners
Your events are listed in the community calendar — the single source of truth for “what's happening this week.” People come for the calendar and discover the creators. You handle tickets your way.
Galleries & cinemas
You invite a painter to exhibit. She's in three communities. Every one of them lights up: “One of our members is exhibiting!” Your gallery fills with visitors who already know and care about the artist. Real engagement, not ad spend.
Workshops & classes
Yoga teachers, cooking classes, pottery studios, language groups — your regulars are already in the community. Post what's coming up. They see it.
Newsletter writers
You write about the scene, the city, the culture. Your newsletter reaches every member of the community. Guaranteed readership, zero pressure.
05

How a painter fills a gallery without a single ad.

→

Clara is a painter. She also takes dance classes, joins a running club, and goes to poetry nights. She's a member of all three communities.

→

People in those communities see her paintings on her blog. They already know her face — they've danced with her, run with her, sat next to her. They become genuine fans.

→

A gallery invites Clara to exhibit. She posts about it on her blog.

→

Every community she's part of lights up: “One of our members is exhibiting at Gallery X!” The runners see it. The dancers see it. The poetry group sees it.

→

The gallery fills with visitors who actually know Clara and care about her work. Not strangers who clicked an ad — real people with a real connection.

This is how the network creates value. Not through algorithms — through the fact that real people in real communities naturally amplify the creative lives of people they already know.

06

How a community works.

→

Someone starts it. A dancer, a gallery owner, a yoga teacher, a group of friends. They become the admin. They create a QR code and choose the first secret password.

→

People join in person. At an event, a class, a meetup. Scan the code, say the password, pick a username. No app store, no sign-up form — the whole thing runs in your browser.

→

Events are the gravity. The community calendar becomes the single source of truth for “what's happening this week.” People come back for the events. They discover the creators along the way.

→

The community governs itself. Members vote on decisions — password changes, new features, how things are run. The admin facilitates, but the community owns it.

→

Communities overlap. A dancer is also in the jazz community. An artist is in three neighborhoods. People bridge communities naturally. The web grows through real human connections.

07

What we don't build

Comments Likes DMs Feeds Followers Notifications Payments Algorithms

If you want to tell someone their work is amazing, you tell them in person. That's not a limitation — it's the entire point.

Under the hood, it's just personal blogs behind a community gate, plus an event calendar. That's why it's simple enough for anyone to run — and why any city can fork it and start their own.

08

“I want to be alone. I want to be with people who know secret things or are also alone.”

— Rainer Maria Rilke, Letters to a Young Poet

A secret password, spoken in person. Never written down. Not in any app, not in any message. No bots can enter. No scrapers. No fake accounts. You were there — that's the only credential that matters.

09

You're not a user.
You're a co-owner.

Each city's social network is a cooperative. You're on the register. You vote on decisions. Creators, readers, event planners, devs — each with a voice proportional to their stake.

The code is open source. If a poetry community in Lisbon wants their own, they fork it. A dog owners' group in Amsterdam? They fork it. A neighborhood collective in Ghent? Fork it.

Grassroot Hopper builds the skeleton. Communities put the flesh on it. Starting with the Lindy Hop community in Brussels — building the community before building the technology.
